# Headings - Reference Note

**Scope note for this skill:** the four heading *types* below (Set order,
Set points, "Even if," Independent) describe argumentative point-headings
used in legal argument sections - each one asserts a legal conclusion.
Factual Background sub-headers must stay neutral and chronological per
Step 11's no-legal-characterization rule, so do not apply these four
types to Factual Background headings. Take only the mechanical guidance
below (nesting, present-tense verbs, signposting) for this skill's
sub-headers. The four types remain here in full because this file is
shared with skills that draft legal argument (Breach, Causation, and
others), where they apply directly.

## How headings are typically organized

Headings are typically organized in one of four ways:

1. **Set order.** According to statutory or common law factors, in
   order: *Plaintiff has established (1) duty, (2) breach, (3)
   causation, and (4) damages.*
2. **Set points.** According to statutory or common law factors, with
   the dispositive one first: *The Court should dismiss this action
   because (1) Plaintiff has not alleged damages.*
3. **"Even if."** Arguing in the alternative, with procedural arguments
   often before substantive ones: *(1) The claim was filed too late, (2)
   Even if the claim had been timely filed, Defendant had no duty.*
4. **Independent.** Self-generated points with the strongest one first:
   *The misstatement was "material" for three reasons. First, it caused
   a 7% drop in share price the very next day. Second, it reflected the
   company's first-quarter profits for one of its main products. Third,
   it exaggerated those profits by 17%.*

## How to nest headings and subheadings

Like how folders contain files that belong to a certain subject, headings
should contain subheadings that are essentially smaller versions of the
main heading. They should be factual and specific, not circular or
topical.

**For Factual Background sub-headers specifically:** a well-drafted
heading tells the reader where the section is going and acts as a
signpost along the way - this nesting discipline and the signposting
purpose transfer directly to a chronological sub-header (e.g. "B.
Deliveries and Emergence of the Defect"), even though the *content* of
that heading must stay factual, not argumentative. Present-tense verbs
give a heading a more conversational feel and are worth using here too.

## How to structure "Even if" arguments

"Even if" arguments are typically in these forms: "Even if the other
side is citing precedent, the case still fails on the merits." "Even if
an exception exists, my client can still prosecute these claims."

### Example: ERISA - Pilot Life Insurance Co. v. Dedeaux

A. ERISA establishes an exclusive federal remedial scheme for disputes
over employee-benefit claims and displaces state-law remedies governing
the same subject.

B. Even if ERISA leaves room for state-law regulation, courts should not
authorize punitive damages because Congress deliberately limited
claimants to the remedies specified in ERISA.
