## General (all matter types)

**Contract execution date.** If a contract's recitals state a date (e.g.
"entered on 1 January 2026") but the signature page shows the parties
signing on different dates, the legally binding date the contract came
into force is the date the **second** party signed — not the date stated
in the recitals. Check the signature page dates independently; don't
take the recital date at face value.

## Renewable Energy

**Expected contract types.** Expect to see an EPC contract or a FIDIC-form
contract. Cite the relevant provisions of these forms directly in the
factual summary rather than paraphrasing generic construction-contract
concepts — FIDIC and EPC forms have their own defined terms and
mechanisms that matter to the drafting.

**Project calendar documents.** Client project teams in this sector
typically maintain detailed contract calendars — tracking when notices
must be sent, when extensions must be filed, when waivers of rights must
be requested. If documents evidencing this calendar (notice logs,
extension requests, waiver correspondence) haven't been provided, flag
their absence — their absence may itself be significant (e.g. a missed
notice deadline), not just an evidentiary gap.

## Joint Ventures

**Milestone mapping.** List every milestone in the venture agreement
together with the consequence attached to it — how each party's rights
and obligations change if the milestone is missed, and what a successful
milestone achievement actually looks like under the contract. Well-drafted
JV agreements define "success" for each milestone explicitly; if the
contract doesn't, flag that ambiguity rather than assuming a definition.

## Software Development

**Evidence-decay risk.** Software development disputes are often litigated
three to five years after the relevant events, by which point the
technical evidence itself may no longer exist or be reproducible — the
software interface, build environment, or system state at the time of
the alleged defect can be effectively unrecoverable, even when both
parties agree on what was originally specified. If evidence needed to
demonstrate a defect (or its absence) is unavailable or impossible to
reproduce, note this explicitly and flag it to the lawyer — it affects
what can actually be proven, not just what happened.
