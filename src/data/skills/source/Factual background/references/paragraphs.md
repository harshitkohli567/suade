# Paragraph Writing

**Scope note for this skill:** the example dialogue and the five
predictable questions below are framed around legal argument (standards,
case law, the other side's points) - useful in full for skills that
draft argument (Breach, Causation, and others). For the Factual
Background section, take the underlying *technique* - open each
paragraph by answering the question the reader is about to ask - and
apply it to a factual question ("what happened next," "why does this
matter to the story," "who did what"), not a legal-standard question.
The mechanics of the dialogue exercise (imagining the reader's next
question and answering it) transfer directly; the specific example
questions below do not.

Start each paragraph by answering a question that you expect the reader
to have.

The way to capture a linear effect in the arguments is to imagine a
dialogue with the judge. Consider this example:

> **Q.** So you want me to deny summary judgment. But the prison doctor
> says Carter has not shown that his treatment fell below the
> professional standard of care.
>
> **A.** Under Carter's Eighth Amendment claim, he must prove that the
> doctor knowingly disregarded a serious medical risk - not that the
> doctor committed medical malpractice.
>
> **Q.** But where do you get the authority to draw that distinction?
>
> **A.** The Supreme Court and the federal courts have long distinguished
> deliberate indifference under the Eighth Amendment from negligence or
> medical malpractice under state tort law.
>
> **Q.** Can you assure me that I will not be reversed if I accept that
> distinction?
>
> **A.** Because the standards are different, courts have denied summary
> judgment where prison officials relied on evidence that their
> treatment was medically defensible but failed to address evidence that
> they knowingly ignored an obvious and substantial risk to the
> prisoner.
>
> **Q.** If you are right, what evidence should I be looking at?
>
> **A.** The relevant question is what the doctor knew about Carter's
> condition and how he responded - not whether another physician might
> have selected a different course of treatment.
>
> **Q.** One last thing: do deliberate-indifference claims and
> malpractice claims not both concern inadequate medical care?
>
> **A.** They may arise from the same treatment, but that does not mean
> they contain the same elements. Malpractice asks whether the doctor
> acted unreasonably; deliberate indifference asks whether the doctor
> consciously disregarded a known and serious risk.
>
> **Q.** So what is the bottom line?
>
> **A.** The doctor cannot obtain summary judgment by recasting Carter's
> constitutional claim as an ordinary dispute over medical negligence.

If you apply this dialoguing technique, each paragraph opener will give
the court a reason the heading is right, answer a question on the
court's mind, and flow from the previous paragraph opener and into the
next.

A good drafter can sense that judges have predictable questions when
reading a section:

1. What's the standard?
2. How does it apply in cases like this one?
3. Which courts have done what you're asking us to do - and why?
4. What about the other side's points?
5. What's the bottom line?
