# Adverse-Fact Detection Protocol

Full sub-process for Step 7 of `SKILL.md`. Consult this before flagging a
fact as adverse.

Identify facts or documents that are sufficiently damaging that omitting
them could make the factual account misleading, incomplete, or
strategically vulnerable. Do not flag every unfavorable fact — flag one
only when it materially weakens an important proposition, supports a
plausible competing explanation, undermines a relied-upon source, or
would cause a reasonable reader to form a materially different impression
if omitted.

## 1. Identify the proposition being advanced

State the key factual claim, inference, or narrative that the draft is
asking the reader to accept — both what it says expressly and what a
reasonable reader would infer.

## 2. Extract the potentially adverse fact

State precisely what the document or evidence establishes. Do not
overstate the evidence. Distinguish between facts, beliefs, predictions,
risks, allegations, and later recollections.

## 3. Determine how the fact affects the narrative

Ask whether the fact:

- directly refutes the proposition;
- materially qualifies it;
- supports a competing explanation;
- changes the chronology;
- shows earlier knowledge or notice;
- undermines intent or causation;
- damages the credibility of a relied-upon witness or source;
- weakens a claim about damages or mitigation.

## 4. Apply the omission test

Ask: **would a reasonable reader form a materially different impression
of the issue if this fact were omitted?** If yes, the fact is likely
material and should not simply be ignored.

## 5. Apply the opponent-use test

Ask: **how would opposing counsel fairly and persuasively use this fact
against the draft?** State the strongest reasonable adverse argument the
evidence actually supports.

## 6. Check context and possible explanations

Before concluding the fact is materially adverse, review the full
document and surrounding communications, dates and chronology, document
versions, authorship and recipients, whether the statement was
conditional, hypothetical, preliminary, or later superseded, and whether
other evidence explains or limits its significance.

## 7. Look for additional evidence

Search for documents that corroborate the adverse fact, contradict it,
explain it, show whether the relevant decision-makers knew about it, or
clarify its significance to the issue being drafted.

## 8. Classify the fact

Use one of the following:

- Immaterially unfavorable
- Contextual qualification
- Material adverse fact
- Central counterfact
- Alternative-story evidence
- Credibility vulnerability
- Omission-risk fact
- Ambiguous; further investigation required

## 9. Recommend treatment

Choose one:

- Omit as immaterial
- Qualify the draft
- Include and contextualize
- Investigate further
- Restructure the factual narrative
- Abandon or revise the unsupported proposition
